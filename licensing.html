The Mission

Something is broken in modern life. Sweet Honey Farm is a serious attempt to build it back.

For most of human history, people lived in small, stable communities where they worked the land together, shared meals, raised children side by side, and carried each other through hardship. Modern life quietly dismantled those structures and renamed the fallout stress, burnout, anxiety, and loneliness.

The U.S. Surgeon General has described loneliness as a public health epidemic. Sweet Honey Farm is not another transactional space designed for consumption. It is a modern village: a place where your absence is noticed, your presence matters, and real relationships form through repeated proximity, shared effort, and work that happens with your hands.

Morganville Is The Proof

People are hungry for something more grounded and real.

What Devon built in Morganville proves the demand is not theoretical. People show up to sweat together, share meals, work the land, care for animals, and become part of a community that feels rare because it asks something real from them.

The opportunity is to make that model replicable without flattening it into a generic lifestyle brand. The goal is an operating system for community: principles, rituals, standards, training, and governance that let independent operators bring the same conditions to life on their own land, in their own regions, with the same seriousness of care.

The standard is the strategy. Growth only makes sense if Devon's values, the member experience, and the feeling of Morganville are protected before expansion is pursued.

The Category

Not a gym. Not a club. Not a retreat center. A modern village with a real operating system.

Land and labor

The farm is not scenery. Animals, gardens, food, movement, weather, tools, and chores create the useful friction that transactional spaces avoid.

Membership with responsibility

Members do not only consume the place. They contribute to it, are known by it, and participate in rituals that make absence visible and presence meaningful.

Standards that travel

The land changes by region. The principles do not: small groups, shared work, careful onboarding, seasonal rhythm, stewardship, and quality control.

Community Architecture

The scaled model can strengthen the community without making it feel engineered.

Stable small groups

As membership grows, each farm can keep people from becoming anonymous by placing them in small recurring circles that notice absence, welcome newcomers, and make care practical.

Shared weekly rhythm

Work days, meals, recovery, family moments, service, seasonal projects, and simple closing rituals can create repeated contact without turning the farm into a programmed club.

Local identity

Every site should feel rooted in its land, people, weather, food, and animals while still carrying the same standards for contribution, hospitality, safety, and stewardship.

Membership Engine

The model turns participation into commitment.

The hard door

Interviews happen on the farm. The first experience involves real work. Membership is selected, capped, and paced so the community can absorb new people without becoming anonymous.

Contribution-indexed pricing

The people carrying the most community labor pay less. The pricing system honors stewardship instead of turning membership into a simple pay-more-get-more hierarchy.

Recurring rhythm

Farm Work Day, shared meals, sauna and cold plunge, small-group check-ins, closing circles, seasonal gatherings, and care reserves make belonging operational rather than sentimental.

The License

The license is not a logo rental. It is the operating architecture that makes the place work.

Founder standards

The brand should scale only with clear concept boundaries, protected methods, operator-facing usage rules, and quality standards that prevent the thing Devon built from being diluted.

Operating playbooks

Site selection, buildout, animal and food safety, member onboarding, staffing, daily rhythm, event programming, incident response, and first-year launch sequence.

Training and accountability

Operator certification, shadow immersion, ongoing review, network reporting, and a standards system built to protect the community before it protects growth.

System Map

The operating system has to cover the whole life of the farm.

Land and buildout

Acreage, access, animal zones, gardens, kitchen, gathering areas, winter capacity, storage, safety routes, and weather plans determine what the community can actually do.

Member design

Interviews, contribution expectations, small-group placement, caps, pricing, financial aid, care reserves, and renewal rules shape who belongs and how seriously they show up.

Ritual calendar

Weekly work, shared meals, movement, recovery, service, seasonal events, family moments, and annual gatherings turn belonging into a repeatable rhythm.

People operations

Farm leadership, hospitality, animal care, food, maintenance, member support, volunteer structure, training, and incident response make the experience durable day after day.

Economics and governance

Membership revenue, operator economics, royalty, brand fund, launch costs, local accountability, reporting cadence, and decision rights keep the mission financially serious.

Quality and risk

Community health metrics, audits, safety standards, insurance, legal review, escalation paths, renewal gates, and brand usage rules protect the model as it grows.

Scale Logic

Fifty farms should mean fifty modern villages, not fifty copies of Morganville.

Operator-led sites

The right operator has local credibility, emotional intelligence, operational discipline, and the willingness to run a demanding land-based community.

Aligned economics

Operators own the local execution and most of the economics. SHF earns through royalty, brand fund, platform support, training, and network infrastructure.

Quality before velocity

Capacity caps, certification, QBRs, community health measurement, legal readiness, and risk controls keep scale from eroding the thing people came for.

The larger opportunity is social infrastructure.

Sweet Honey Farm is a chance to rebuild something modern life quietly eroded: places where people are known, useful, physically present, and responsible to one another. The business model matters because it lets the work endure. The mission matters because it explains why the business deserves to exist.

The design bar for fifty sites should be that, for many members, the farm becomes one of the most meaningful community experiences of their lives: a place where they are known, needed, challenged, and supported through repeated shared work.

Next Steps

The next phase is analysis and operating-system refinement with Devon and the SHF team.

Anthropological survey

Observe the original farm across ordinary days, member rituals, work blocks, meals, staff interactions, family moments, and seasonal events. Interview Devon, the SHF team, members, and waitlist prospects to identify the behaviors that create belonging.

Operational review

Work with Devon and the SHF team to map how Morganville actually runs: staffing, animals, food, facilities, safety, scheduling, member onboarding, maintenance, incidents, costs, bottlenecks, and the judgment calls currently carried by the founder and team.

OS refinement

Translate the analysis into a sharper operating system: standards, member journey, community practices, staffing model, training path, quality measures, economics, legal guardrails, and the launch sequence for future farms.

Expansion readiness

Decide with Devon and the SHF team what must be true before anything scales: operator profile, land requirements, member cap, review cadence, brand controls, support model, and the conditions that would justify saying no.
